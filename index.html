Astronomers Directly Measure Mass of Saturn-Size Rogue Planet for First Time

International team uses Earth and space observatories simultaneously to detect free-floating world 9,800 light-years away.

An international team led by Peking University's Subo Dong announced the discovery of a new rogue planet drifting among the stars without orbiting any sun. The Saturn-mass world, located about 9,800 light-years away toward the galactic center, represents the first rogue planet detected using both ground-based and space-based observatories simultaneously. More significantly, it's the first time astronomers have directly measured a rogue planet's mass. Scientists estimate billions or even trillions of such free-floating worlds may exist in our galaxy alone—possibly outnumbering planets that orbit stars.

Critical Details:

  • Detection used ESA's Gaia telescope and multiple ground surveys
  • Few dozen confirmed and candidate rogue planets currently known
  • Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope expected to discover 400+ more by 2027
  • Rogue planets difficult to find due to darkness (no star illumination)
  • Discovery published in Science journal's first 2026 issue
  • May shed light on planetary system formation processes

Scientists Watch Plants Breathe in Real Time With New "Stomata In-Sight" System

University of Illinois breakthrough could unlock drought-resistant crops by observing microscopic pores while measuring gas exchange.

Researchers at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign created a powerful system that allows scientists to watch plants breathe—live and in high definition—while tracking exactly how much carbon and water they exchange with air. The tool, called "Stomata In-Sight," overcomes a major obstacle by observing minute movements of stomata (microscopic pores on leaves) while simultaneously measuring gas exchange under controlled conditions. The breakthrough could help develop crops that grow with less water while reliably producing food, biofuel, and bioproducts, especially critical for drought-prone regions.

Critical Details:

  • Stomata act as adjustable valves controlling CO2 intake and water vapor release
  • Number and behavior of stomata directly affect plant water efficiency
  • Published in journal Plant Physiology
  • Could accelerate breeding of climate-adapted crop varieties
  • Essential for food security in regions facing water scarcity
  • Provides unprecedented detail on photosynthesis tradeoffs
