NASA Makes Unprecedented Early Return Decision for ISS Crew
First time in 65 years of human spaceflight that NASA has cut a mission short due to astronaut illness. Crew-11 to return within days.

NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman announced Thursday that the four-person Crew-11 mission will return to Earth ahead of schedule after one crew member experienced an undisclosed medical situation on January 7. The affected astronaut is stable but requires evaluation and treatment available only on Earth. This marks the first time in NASA's 65-year history of human spaceflight that a mission has been cut short due to crew illness.

The decision leaves only three people aboard the ISS, significantly impacting station operations and science experiments. Crew-11 members—NASA astronauts Zena Cardman and Mike Fincke, JAXA astronaut Kimiya Yui, and Roscosmos cosmonaut Oleg Platonov—launched in August 2025 and were scheduled to remain until around May 2026. A planned spacewalk was cancelled, and NASA is evaluating whether to accelerate the launch of Crew-12, currently scheduled for mid-February.

Key Details
  • Historic first: NASA has never previously ended a mission early for medical reasons
  • Crew member stable but requires ground-based medical evaluation
  • SpaceX Crew Dragon capsule to undock Wednesday, splashdown Thursday off California coast
  • Only three crew members will remain on ISS, limiting operations

Brain-Inspired AI Architectures May Learn Without Massive Training Data
Johns Hopkins study challenges dominant AI strategy, showing biologically-inspired neural networks can mimic brain activity before seeing any training data.

Research from Johns Hopkins University published in Nature Machine Intelligence demonstrates that artificial intelligence systems built with brain-inspired architectures can begin resembling human brain activity even before training on any data. The findings challenge the current AI industry approach of throwing enormous datasets and computing resources at problems, suggesting architectural design may be as important as data volume.

Lead author Mick Bonner notes that while the AI field is "spending hundreds of billions of dollars" on compute resources "the size of small cities" and months of training on massive datasets, humans learn to see using very little data. The research highlights that starting with a brain-like architectural foundation could provide an alternative path to current brute-force scaling strategies that dominate the industry.
